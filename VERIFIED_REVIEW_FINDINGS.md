### 2. Source Misattribution — IDB/World Bank

In Section 1.2, the text reads: "the Inter-American Development Bank estimates disability prevalence in Guyana at 13.6 per cent, consistent with the WHO global estimate of approximately 15 per cent (World Bank, 2024)."

The problem: the 13.6% figure is attributed in the text to the IDB but cited to the World Bank. These are different organisations. Either:
- Restructure the sentence so that it attributes the reporting to the World Bank (which aggregates international data) without specifying "Inter-American Development Bank" in the text, or
- Add the IDB as a separate reference if the exact IDB source can be identified.

The same issue recurs in Recommendation 4 (Section 5.4), where "IDB estimates suggest a figure closer to 13.6% for Guyana" is followed by "(World Bank, 2024)."

### 3. Armstrong Reference Type Mismatch

The body text (Section 2.4.5) treats Armstrong, Armstrong and Spandagou (2005) as a standalone empirical study ("documented that across the Eastern Caribbean, the curriculum for children with disabilities remained heavily biased toward vocational skills"). The reference list correctly identifies it as a book chapter in Mitchell (ed.), *Contextualizing inclusive education*. The body text should acknowledge this is a book chapter, not a journal article.

### 4. Generic Online References

Two reference list entries are too vague for the specific claims made from them:
- "Ministry of Education Guyana (n.d.) Inclusive education initiatives in Guyana [Online]. Available at: https://education.gov.gy" — this is a homepage, not a specific document.
- "World Bank (2024) Disability inclusion in Guyana: Country profile [Online]. Available at: https://www.worldbank.org/en/topic/disability" — this is a general topic page, not a specific country profile.

These should either be made more specific (with exact URLs to the documents cited) or the claims sourced from them should be attributed to more precise sources.

## MODERATE ISSUES

### 8. Prose Style — AI Detection Risk

One reviewer identified specific passages with over-symmetrical construction:
- The abstract uses a highly regular sequence: "The study… The central proposition… A structured comparison… An operationalisation matrix… The study concludes…"
- Vocabulary concentration: "operationalisation," "institutional authority," "partial and uneven institutionalisation" recur heavily
- Balanced hedging pattern throughout: positive finding → immediate qualification → reframing
- Sections 5.3 (conclusions) and the abstract are the most formulaic passages

These are style concerns, not errors, but they increase AI detection risk. Targeted de-patterning in the abstract and Section 5.3 is advisable.

### 9. SEN vs SEND Terminology

The dissertation analyses a policy framed in SEN terms but draws on recent materials using SEND language. Both are used in the text. A brief note acknowledging this terminological shift (e.g., that Guyana's policy uses "SEN" while recent CPCE programmes use "SEND") would resolve any appearance of inconsistency.

### 10. Direct Quotations from Policy Definitions

One reviewer noted that direct quotations from the Draft Policy's definitions section (Jamaica, Kenya, UK definitions of SEN) should include page numbers per Harvard convention. The Definitions section starts at p. 6 per the policy TOC.
