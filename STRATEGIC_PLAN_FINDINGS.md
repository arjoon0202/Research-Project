## 1. Overall Vision and Policy Framework

The ESP's 2030 Vision is: "Providing opportunities for quality, equitable education and lifelong learning for all" (Part 2, p. 77; Part 3, p. 138). The ultimate goal is "To contribute to employability and reduction of poverty, by increasing performance at all levels and reducing the disparity between sub-groups" (Part 2, p. 77).

Equity is one of five intermediate outcomes (Intermediate Outcome 4: "Reducing inequities in education") and is described as a "cross-cutting" concern (Part 1, p. 3; Part 2, p. 81).

The plan identifies five strategic goals/intermediate outcomes (Part 2, p. 77):
1. Improving governance and accountability
2. Improving performance at all levels
3. Improving the efficiency of the education system
4. Reducing inequities in education
5. Contributing to lifelong learning and employability

## 2. Special Education — Structural Overview

### 2.1 SEN Unit
The Special Education Needs Unit (SEN Unit) was "established in 2009" and "gives concrete expression to the national education policy on inclusion (Lockwood 2010). This Unit centralises and leads efforts in Special Education." (Part 1, p. 17)

### 2.2 Ministry Policy on Mainstreaming
"The overarching policy of the Ministry is to cater for students with disabilities in mainstream schools. Although Special schools exist, new policy objectives also aim at improving and expanding education services, sometimes in collaboration with NGOs, to cater for these students." (Part 1, p. 16)

### 2.3 Special Education Section
"While the stated policy of the Ministry is to, as far as possible, mainstream students with disabilities, there are special schools which cater to students with a range of physical, sensory and learning disabilities. The Ministry's full embrace of Special Education Needs is guided by its new policy directive, evidenced in the establishment and resourcing of a Unit to guide and drive the process." (Part 1, p. 17)

"It is recognised that students who are gifted or those displaying learning deficiencies require special education provision. Beyond the allocation of budgetary resources, the challenge is to ensure that the paradigm shift changes the way teaching is undertaken and success is defined. This is a long term enterprise for it is well known that the citadel of prejudices against special needs students and those with disabilities is not easily conquered. The goal is the removal of inequities in education." (Part 1, p. 17)

## 3. Access and Participation of Students with Disabilities

### 3.1 Data Challenges
"Anecdotal information for many years suggested that students with disabilities start school later, have the most difficulty getting access to a school, have the lowest participation rates at all levels and drop out at the highest rates. There was however a serious lack of sound data on the actual numbers, type of disability and what specifically are the challenges faced by these students because there has been no proper survey of persons with disabilities for many years." (Part 2, p. 42–43)

### 3.2 National Commission on Disability Survey Findings
"There has been some improvement over the last year because of a major survey of persons with disabilities in five regions (2, 3, 5, 6 and 10) undertaken by the National Commission on Disability." (Part 2, p. 43)

Key findings from this survey:
- "In all five regions, an average of 11% of those persons with disabilities (PWDs) who were over 18 had never attended school." (Part 2, p. 43)
- Four main reasons cited: "not finding an institution willing to take them; parents did not encourage or allow them; they did not think they would be able to learn, and finance and transport difficulties." (Part 2, p. 43)
- "Nearly 50% of this group (between 45% and 49%) had only attended primary school, less than 20% attended secondary and less than 5% went on to any form of post-secondary or tertiary education." (Part 2, p. 43)
- "Financial difficulties and transportation were cited as the most serious challenges, and transportation difficulties are often related to finance. Other challenges were difficulty finding a school and the attitude of fellow students." (Part 2, p. 43)
- "The attitude of teachers and physical access were also cited as challenges though not as frequently." (Part 2, p. 43)

### 3.3 Enrolment Data
The Ministry "routinely collects data from the Special schools which cater specifically for students with disabilities and which over the years have had an enrollment of about 400 students." (Part 2, p. 42–43)

"The major exceptions [to primary enrolment] would be those children with disabilities and children who live in very remote/scattered communities." (Part 2, p. 37)

## 4. Inclusive Education — Policy Priorities and Strategies

### 4.1 Intermediate Outcome 4: Reducing Inequities in Education

"The main challenges in the system, as it relates to this issue, are the lack of resources and access to quality education service delivery across subgroups (students living in the hinterland and remote riverine areas, students who have a different first language and students with a disability)." (Part 2, p. 81)

"The failure of students with disabilities to perform well, and for many to move beyond the primary level, even when they do not have a learning disability, reduces the capacity of these persons to earn a reasonable living and contribute to national development." (Part 2, p. 81)

**Strategies under this outcome:**
"The MOE plans to specifically address inequity by instituting strategies that foster inclusivity. These strategies include but are not limited to, development/establishment of standards on materials to be procured for special education needs and disability programmes/facilities. This will enable the Ministry to improve the service delivery offered to children with special education needs." (Part 2, p. 81)

### 4.2 Targets Related to Disability
From the ESP targets table (Part 2, p. 77):
- "Percentage change in students with sensory disabilities participating in national and regional assessments by 2025"
- Performance index target: 0.93 for hinterland versus coastal students by 2025

### 4.3 SDG Alignment
The plan aligns with SDG 4.5: "eliminate gender disparities in education and ensure equal access to all levels of education and vocational training for the vulnerable, including persons with disabilities, indigenous peoples and children in vulnerable situations" (Part 3, p. 87)

SDG 4.a: "Build and upgrade education facilities that are child, disability and gender sensitive and provide safe, non-violent, inclusive and effective learning environments for all" (Part 3, p. 87)

### 4.4 CARICOM HRD Strategy Alignment
The plan lists as a priority: "Enhance attention to our children with Special Education Needs (SEN)" (Part 3, p. 87)

## 5. SEND Provision — Detailed Strategies (Strategy 4.1.1)

The ESP contains a comprehensive strategy titled **"4.1.1 Enhance and improve SEND delivery"** (Part 3, pp. 111–112, 155–156). The responsible unit is the **"SEND Unit"** (Part 3, p. 112).

### 5.1 Key Activities Under Strategy 4.1.1
(All from Part 3, pp. 111–112)

1. **4.1.1.1** — Facilitate the improvement of educational infrastructure to cater to SEND students, through the development of SEND accessible standards
2. **4.1.1.2** — Develop standards/general list of assistive technologies for SEND
3. **4.1.1.3** — Facilitate the establishment of SEND space within existing schools based on identified needs
4. **4.1.1.4** — Collaborate with nursery sector to facilitate parent circle groups
5. **4.1.1.5** — Facilitate screening of students at all levels
6. **4.1.1.6** — Develop IEP for screened students through the Regional SEND Diagnostic and Treatment Centre
7. **4.1.1.7** — Publicize guidelines for students accessing educational opportunities
8. **4.1.1.8** — Develop and standardise quality and type of support for SEND
9. **4.1.1.9** — Train teachers to conduct initial screening at all levels through the use of a standardised instrument
10. **4.1.1.10** — Conduct CPD based on needs, but with particular emphasis on the needs of students in the Hinterland regions. This will include continuation of training in sign language, techniques to deal with autistic students, etc.
11. **4.1.1.11** — Develop alternative methods of assessment for children with SEND
12. **4.1.1.12** — Facilitate parental education programmes, sensitization and support for SEND students
13. **4.1.1.13** — Collaborate with TVET Unit to conduct a situation analysis of SEND in Guyana under the employability project

### 5.2 Terminological Note: SEN vs SEND
The ESP uses both "SEN" and "SEND." The earlier plan period (2014–2018) consistently uses "SEN" (e.g., "SEN Unit," "SEN children," "SEN students"). The 2021–2025 plan uses "SEND" for the forward-looking strategies (e.g., "Enhance and improve SEND delivery," "SEND Unit," "SEND accessible standards," "children with SEND"). The policy framework review is still labelled "Review the Special Education Needs Policy Framework" (using SEN), while operational strategies use SEND. This reflects an evolution in terminology within the planning period.

## 6. Teacher Training for SEN/SEND

### 6.1 CPCE — Cyril Potter College of Education
"CPCE offers initial teacher training at the nursery, primary and secondary (academic and pre-vocational) levels." (Part 1, p. 17)

The ESP includes strategy **1.1.7**: "Improve the quality of teaching by aligning the training at CPCE with the new curriculum" with activities including "1.1.7.1 Review and revise the CPCE curriculum by aligning it with the national curriculum reform programme" and "1.1.7.2 Conduct training of teacher educators to deliver the revised curriculum" (Part 3, p. 102).

### 6.2 SEN Teacher Training — Specific Activities
- **2.4.1.1** — "Collaborate with the Ministry of Public Health, Special Needs Unit, & Welfare Unit to conduct training of teachers in the identification of areas for screening" (Part 3, p. 107)
- **2.4.1.2** — "Conduct training of current and future secondary school teachers to identify learning deficiencies and disabilities" (Part 3, p. 107)
- **3.1.2.1** — "Revise training manual for screening activities" (Part 3, p. 108)
- **3.1.2.2** — "Train teachers to conduct early identification screening activities" (Part 3, p. 108)
- **4.1.1.9** — "Train teachers to conduct initial screening at all levels through the use of a standardised instrument" (Part 3, p. 112)
- **4.1.1.10** — "Conduct CPD based on needs, but with particular emphasis on the needs of students in the Hinterland regions. This will include continuation of training in sign language, techniques to deal with autistic students, etc." (Part 3, p. 112)

## 7. Monitoring and Evaluation

### 7.1 M&E Framework
"Monitoring and Evaluation have become an integral management tool of the Ministry of Education, in its efforts to ensure accountability and transparency." (Part 3, p. 138)

The M&E system uses "Open Education Management Information System (Open EMIS)" for monitoring (Part 3, p. 138).

Three levels of monitoring are specified (Part 3, pp. 138–139):
- **Routine Monitoring:** Quarterly reporting with two key reporting sessions per year
- **Annual Reviews:** Conducted by the committees, with yearly expenditure reports
- **Mid-term Reviews:** Biennial, to assess likelihood of achieving strategic objectives

### 7.2 M&E Indicators for SEND
The results framework (Part 3, pp. 155–156) specifies the following SEND-related indicators:
- "% of teachers trained to conduct initial screening"
- "% of students screened"
- "# of parents who benefit from SEND programmes"
- "% of students accessing national examinations through alternative assessment methods"
- "MOU/clear standards on types of support acceptable for SEND from external agencies developed"
- "New non-academic standards for SEND revised/developed"
- "Benchmarks developed"
- "SEND standards developed"

Baseline data (2017–2018): 0% for teachers trained in screening, 0% for students accessing alternative assessment, and "A draft document exists" for the SEND policy.

## 8. School Infrastructure and Physical Access

"New schools are still being built without access for children with physical disabilities and with poor designs that make them hot and uncomfortable for a good part of the day." (Part 2, p. 73)

The ESP commits to "establish building standards for schools to cater for students with physical disabilities, in its effort to improve school climate and expand access to the most vulnerable." (Part 2, p. 75)

Strategy 3.2.8.1: "Construct secondary schools in Regions 3 and 4, with facilities that cater to students with disability" (Part 3, p. 110)

Strategy 3.1.1.7: "Advocate for specialised transportation for children with disability" (Part 3, p. 108)

## 9. Budget and Resource Allocation

### 9.1 Overall Budget Approach
"The Ministry developed an Education Resource Project tool with technical assistance from the World Bank in 2014." Projections were "moderate" — "an average of 5.6% growth in the first two years of the plan and 10-28% in the last three years. Education expenditure as a % of GDP is expected to increase to almost 6% by the end of the plan period." (Part 3, p. 88)

### 9.2 Cost for Intermediate Outcome 4 (Reducing Inequities)
"Projected outcome cost ('000) – (G$10,755,701)." (Part 2, p. 81)

This is the total projected cost for Programme 4.1 (Ensure equitable distribution of education resources and delivery across education districts), which includes SEND delivery, school feeding, and first-language policy initiatives. The SEND component is not separately itemised within this figure.

### 9.3 CPCE Teacher Training Cost
From Part 3 (p. 126), the overall cost for the teacher training strategy for the plan period (3 years) includes G$324,000 ('000) for CPCE curriculum alignment and G$10,000 ('000) for related consultation activities.

## 10. Previous Plan Period Achievements (2014–2018)

### 10.1 SEN-Related Activities in Previous Plan
The previous plan period included (Part 1, pp. 21–34):
- **Initiative 2.2:** "Develop alternative methods of assessment depending on methodologies used to teach the subject and for students who have special education needs"
- **Initiative 5.3:** "As an integral part of its curricula reform, the Ministry will ensure that curricula are adapted for children with special education needs" — Status: "The curriculum started at the end of the plan period; this initiative is on-going."
- **Initiative 6.5:** "The SEN and School Health Units will collaborate in identifying children with SEN through screening and in providing educational support, for example, transportation and hiring of teacher aides."

### 10.2 Results of Previous Plan
- "Intermediate Outcome 6 – Students' instructional time increases: 39% of this was completed. Major achievements were: the school feeding programmes were expanded to include breakfast; a database to track at-risk students in need of support was established, and the Regional Special Education Needs/Disabilities Diagnostic and Treatment Centre was established to provide individualised support for children with special needs." (Part 1, p. 34)
- Targets for teacher aides: "Number of teacher aides for special schools increased by 10%" (Year 3), building to "Number of teacher aides for special schools and schools with children with SEN increased by 30%" (Year 5) (Part 1, pp. 33–34)

### 10.3 Percentage of SEN Children in National Assessments
Listed as an indicator under the 2014–2018 plan with baseline "No baseline information is available at this time" (Part 1, p. 21). This indicator was not implemented during the previous plan period.
