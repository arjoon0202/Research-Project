# Additional Annotated Bibliography — Supplementary Sources for Dissertation Revision

## Inclusive Education in Guyana: A Desktop Analysis of the Draft Inclusion Policy

**Purpose:** This supplementary annotated bibliography identifies and annotates additional sources not included in the original reference list that would strengthen the dissertation's empirical grounding, particularly in four areas: (1) Guyana-specific and Caribbean-specific inclusive education research; (2) policy implementation theory beyond Pressman and Wildavsky; (3) qualitative methodology sources — especially on working hypotheses in qualitative research; and (4) international normative instruments not yet cited. Each entry provides the full reference, source type, overview, key themes, and specific relevance to the dissertation's research questions, hypotheses, and identified revision needs.

## Section A: Guyana-Specific Empirical Sources

### 1. Ajodhia-Andrews, A. and Frankel, E. (2010) 'Inclusive education in Guyana: A call for change', *International Journal of Special Education*, 25(1), pp. 126–144.

**Source Type:** Peer-reviewed journal article (empirical, qualitative)

**Overview:** This study examines inclusive education in Guyana for children with special needs aged zero to eight years, drawing on the perspectives of policymakers, teachers, and parents (n = 22). The study uses a social-constructivist framework and grounded theory methodology for data collection and analysis.

**Key Themes:** Four themes emerged as potential barriers to inclusive education in Guyana: attitudes and perceptions toward those with special needs; change agents; resources; and experiences with children with special needs. The study identifies the interrelationship between these factors and argues that attitudinal barriers — particularly among parents and educators — are the core phenomenon obstructing sustainable inclusion in Guyana.

**Relevance to the Study:** This is the most directly relevant empirical study available on inclusive education in Guyana. It provides primary qualitative data from Guyanese stakeholders that the dissertation currently lacks. Its identification of attitudinal barriers as a core phenomenon would significantly enrich the discussion in Chapter 4 (particularly Section 4.4) and provide empirical counter-evidence or complementary evidence to the structural-institutional focus of the current analysis. It is relevant to Research Questions 2 and 3, and to Subsidiary Hypotheses 2 and 3.

### 2. Cheong, K.A., Kellems, R.O., Andersen, M.M. and Steed, K. (2019) 'The education of individuals with disabilities in Guyana: An overview', *Intervention in School and Clinic*, 54(4), pp. 246–250.

**Source Type:** Peer-reviewed journal article (descriptive overview)

**Overview:** This article provides a historical background and summary of current practices related to the identification, educational placements, and provision of educational services and supports for students with disabilities in Guyana. It draws on the NCD survey data and government sources.

**Key Themes:** The article documents that despite significant improvements in Guyana's education system over the past two decades, students with disabilities still face higher rates of poverty, lack of access to schools, and a shortage of trained teachers. It references the Persons with Disabilities Act of 2010 and the Draft SEN Inclusion Policy as key legislative and policy instruments. The NCD survey data reported that 15% of individuals with disabilities had never attended school, and among children under 16, that figure was 42%. The article also notes that 52% of those who had never attended school cited parental discouragement as a factor.

**Relevance to the Study:** This source provides critical empirical data on disability prevalence and educational exclusion in Guyana that the dissertation's literature review (Chapter 2, Section 2.4) currently lacks. The NCD survey statistics would strengthen the problem statement in Chapter 1 and provide a quantitative evidence base for the qualitative analysis in Chapter 4. It is directly relevant to Research Question 3 and the Central Hypothesis.

### 3. Lashley, L. (2021) 'Stay in your lane: Experiences of children with Special Education Needs and/or Disabilities in two mainstream primary schools in Guyana', *International Journal of Inclusive Education*, 27(8), pp. 921–971.

**Source Type:** Peer-reviewed journal article (empirical, qualitative ethnographic)

**Overview:** This article presents findings from six months of ethnographic research in two mainstream primary schools in Guyana. Using situational analysis through a poststructuralist lens with the social model of disability theoretical framework, Lashley examines the lived experiences of children with SEND placed in mainstream settings.

**Key Themes:** The study documents experiences of marginalisation, discrimination, and depersonalisation among children with SEND in rural mainstream primary schools. The metaphor of "staying in your lane" captures the systemic nature of the exclusion experienced, where children with SEND are effectively told — through institutional practices, teacher behaviour, and peer dynamics — that mainstream schooling is not for them. The study provides direct evidence that formal placement in mainstream schools without systemic support constitutes what Slee (2011) describes as integration without inclusion.

**Relevance to the Study:** This is the most recent and methodologically rigorous empirical study of inclusive education practice in Guyana. It provides the ground-level evidence of the policy-practice gap that the dissertation identifies through document analysis alone. Lashley's findings directly corroborate and extend the dissertation's analysis in Section 4.4.2 (absence of disability-specific guidance) by showing what happens in practice when broad policy directives meet classroom realities. It is essential for Research Question 3 and would significantly strengthen the evidence base for Subsidiary Hypotheses 2 and 3.

### 4. Lashley, L. (2023) 'The curious gap in the experiences of children with exceptionalities in two mainstream primary schools in postcolonial Guyana', *International Journal of Inclusive Education*, 29(6), pp. 935–957.

**Source Type:** Peer-reviewed journal article (empirical, qualitative ethnographic)

**Overview:** This companion article extends Lashley's ethnographic research into the learning and socialising experiences and opportunities of children with SEND in the same two mainstream primary schools. The data were analysed using situational analysis as posited by Adele Clarke and interpreted through a poststructuralist lens.

**Key Themes:** The study reveals that children with SEND find mainstream schools challenging environments marked by marginalisation and discrimination. It extends the "stay in your lane" discourse into an analysis of the gap between policy aspiration and lived experience — what Lashley terms the "curious gap between hope and happening." The study contributes a "southern inclusive framework" embedded in the social model of disability, contextualised for Guyana.

**Relevance to the Study:** Lashley's concept of the "curious gap" is a powerful empirical articulation of the same policy-practice gap that the dissertation's Central Hypothesis identifies. The "southern inclusive framework" also provides a postcolonial corrective to the dissertation's reliance on predominantly Northern/Western scholarly frameworks. It is relevant to Research Questions 2 and 3, and to the conceptual framework discussion in Chapter 2.

### 5. Lashley, L. (2024) 'The inclusive education: A conceptualized reality or misapprehension in postcolonial Guyana', *International Journal of Inclusive Education* [published online].

**Source Type:** Peer-reviewed journal article (theoretical/empirical)

**Overview:** This article examines the power dynamics in mainstream schools in Guyana, highlighting resistance and opposition to the placement of children with SEND. It extends the discourse on the sociocultural experiences of children with SEND in postcolonial Guyana.

**Key Themes:** Lashley argues that inclusive education in Guyana remains firmly held in the tenets of the medical model of disability, despite policy language that invokes social model principles. The article documents a formal zero-rejection policy that is not actively practised, with children with SEND routinely told that mainstream schools cannot accommodate them. It positions these practices within the colonial legacy of Guyana's education system.

**Relevance to the Study:** This source directly challenges the dissertation's finding that the Draft Inclusion Policy reflects social model thinking (Section 4.2). Lashley's evidence suggests that regardless of policy language, the system's actual operation remains rooted in medical model assumptions. This would provide important counter-evidence for a revised Chapter 4 and would complicate the currently uniform "confirmed" verdicts on the hypotheses.

**Relevance to the Study:** This source provides evidence of a specific institutional gap — transition services — that the Draft Inclusion Policy does not address. It would strengthen the analysis of structural factors in Section 4.4 and contribute to the discussion of operational specificity gaps identified in the analysis.

## Section B: Caribbean-Specific Inclusive Education Research

### 8. Blackman, S.N.J., Conrad, D.A. and Brown, L.I. (eds.) (2019) *Achieving Inclusive Education in the Caribbean and Beyond: From Philosophy to Praxis*. Cham: Springer.

**Source Type:** Edited academic volume

**Overview:** This edited volume offers an international perspective on philosophical, conceptual, and praxis-oriented issues that impinge on achieving education for all students, with a primary focus on the Caribbean region.

**Key Themes:** The book examines historical, systemic, structural, organisational, and attitudinal barriers that continue to be antithetical to the philosophy and practice of inclusive education within the Caribbean. It addresses how globalised views of inclusion informed by philosophical ideas from the Global North have influenced and continue to influence the equity in education agenda in the region. The second section considers how exclusion and marginalisation still occur across selected Caribbean islands.

**Relevance to the Study:** This is the most comprehensive edited collection on inclusive education in the Caribbean. Its Caribbean-specific theoretical framing would strengthen the dissertation's literature review by providing regional comparative context currently absent from Chapter 2. It is relevant to all four research questions and would address the criticism that the dissertation relies too heavily on Northern/Western scholarly frameworks.

### 9. Armstrong, A.C., Armstrong, D. and Spandagou, I. (2005) 'Special and inclusive education in the Eastern Caribbean: Policy, practice and provision', *International Journal of Inclusive Education*, 9(1), pp. 71–87.

**Source Type:** Peer-reviewed journal article (empirical, comparative)

**Overview:** This article discusses research on the implementation of the common educational reform strategy in Eastern Caribbean countries, reporting on barriers to inclusive education that persist in the region.

**Key Themes:** The study found that five of six participating countries were developing education plans including policies for children with special needs. However, barriers persisted, including a heavy bias toward vocational skills for students with disabilities, insufficient special education teachers, and inadequate resource allocation. The curriculum for children with disabilities was largely related to functionality rather than academic achievement.

**Relevance to the Study:** This source provides valuable regional comparative context showing that the structural factors identified in the Guyana analysis — particularly the gap between policy aspiration and implementation, and insufficient teacher preparation — are characteristic of the wider Caribbean region. This would contextualise Guyana's experience within a regional pattern for Chapter 2 and Chapter 4.

### 10. Conrad, D.A. and Brown, L.I. (2011) 'Fostering inclusive education: Principals' perspectives in Trinidad and Tobago', *International Journal of Inclusive Education*, 15(9), pp. 1017–1029.

**Source Type:** Peer-reviewed journal article (empirical, qualitative)

**Overview:** This study examines school principals' perspectives and experiences regarding inclusive education in Trinidad and Tobago, focusing on the role of school leadership in fostering or hindering inclusive practices.

**Key Themes:** Findings point to the need to place social justice at the centre of Trinidad and Tobago's school improvement agenda. Principals identified lack of resources, inadequate training, and attitudinal barriers as key challenges. The study demonstrates that leadership commitment to inclusion is a necessary but insufficient condition without systemic support.

**Relevance to the Study:** This Caribbean-specific study of school leadership and inclusion provides a useful comparator for the Guyanese context and would strengthen the discussion in Section 4.4.3 on professional development. It demonstrates that the challenges identified in Guyana's Draft Inclusion Policy are shared across Caribbean education systems.

### 11. Srivastava, M., de Boer, A.A. and Pijl, S.J. (2015) 'Inclusive education in developing countries: A closer look at its implementation in the last 10 years', *Educational Review*, 67(2), pp. 179–195.

**Source Type:** Peer-reviewed journal article (systematic literature review)

**Overview:** This systematic review examines projects undertaken to support the inclusion of students with disabilities in developing countries, focusing on four implementation factors: external factors, school factors, teacher factors, and parent factors.

**Key Themes:** The review finds that implemented projects focused mainly on school and teacher factors, with only two studies reporting on their effects. Serious gaps in implementing inclusive education in developing countries persist, particularly in monitoring and evaluating the impact of inclusion initiatives.

**Relevance to the Study:** This systematic review provides an important evidence base for the dissertation's analysis of monitoring and accountability gaps (Section 4.4.4). Its finding that few inclusion projects in developing countries measure their own effects directly supports the argument that the absence of monitoring systems is a structural barrier.

## Section C: Policy Implementation Theory

### 12. Matland, R.E. (1995) 'Synthesizing the implementation literature: The ambiguity-conflict model of policy implementation', *Journal of Public Administration Research and Theory*, 5(2), pp. 145–174.

**Source Type:** Peer-reviewed journal article (theoretical/conceptual)

**Overview:** This influential article synthesises the policy implementation literature by proposing an ambiguity-conflict model that reconciles top-down and bottom-up approaches to implementation analysis.

**Key Themes:** Matland identifies four implementation paradigms based on the degree of policy ambiguity and conflict: administrative implementation (low ambiguity, low conflict); political implementation (low ambiguity, high conflict); experimental implementation (high ambiguity, low conflict); and symbolic implementation (high ambiguity, high conflict). The model predicts that policies with high ambiguity and low conflict — which plausibly describes the Draft Inclusion Policy — tend toward experimental implementation, where outcomes vary by local conditions and contextual factors.

**Relevance to the Study:** This model would substantially strengthen the conceptual framework in Chapter 2 and the analysis in Chapter 4. The Draft Inclusion Policy is arguably a case of "symbolic implementation" — high ambiguity (broad aspirational language without operational specificity) combined with low overt conflict (general endorsement of inclusion without contested resource allocation). Matland's framework would provide a more sophisticated theoretical account of why the policy-practice gap persists than Pressman and Wildavsky's linear implementation deficit model alone.

### 13. Ball, S.J. (1993) 'What is policy? Texts, trajectories and toolboxes', *Discourse: Studies in the Cultural Politics of Education*, 13(2), pp. 10–17.

**Source Type:** Peer-reviewed journal article (theoretical)

**Overview:** This influential article argues that policy should be understood both as text and as discourse. Ball distinguishes between policy as text (the written document, subject to interpretation) and policy as discourse (the broader framework that shapes what is thinkable and sayable about education).

**Key Themes:** Ball argues that policy is not simply implemented but is reinterpreted, adapted, and transformed at every level of the system. He critiques linear models of policy implementation that assume a straightforward transmission from policy text to practice. The concept of "policy trajectory" — the idea that policy moves through different contexts of production, influence, and practice — offers a more dynamic account of the policy process.

**Relevance to the Study:** Ball's policy-as-text/policy-as-discourse distinction would significantly strengthen the theoretical framing of the document analysis methodology. Currently, the dissertation treats the Draft Inclusion Policy primarily as a prescriptive text that either is or is not implemented. Ball's framework would enable a more sophisticated analysis of how the policy's language constructs particular understandings of inclusion, disability, and educational provision. Note: Ball, Maguire and Braun (2012) is already in the reference list but is not engaged with substantively in the analysis.

## Section D: Qualitative Methodology — Working Hypotheses

### 14. Lincoln, Y.S. and Guba, E.G. (1985) *Naturalistic Inquiry*. Newbury Park: Sage Publications.

**Source Type:** Authored methodological text

**Overview:** This foundational text on qualitative research methodology proposes a naturalistic paradigm as an alternative to the rationalistic paradigm that dominates conventional social science inquiry. It articulates the axioms, methods, and quality criteria appropriate to naturalistic inquiry.

**Key Themes:** Lincoln and Guba articulate the concept of "working hypotheses" — tentative, context-bound propositions that guide but do not determine the inquiry. Unlike formal hypotheses in positivist research, working hypotheses in naturalistic inquiry are understood as provisional, evolving through engagement with data, and transferable only as "time- and context-bound" understandings rather than as universal generalisations. The distinction between working hypotheses and formal hypotheses is methodologically critical: the former are refined through the analytical process, while the latter are tested against predetermined criteria.

**Relevance to the Study:** This source is essential for the methodological revision needed in Chapter 3. The dissertation currently uses hypotheses in a manner closer to positivist hypothesis-testing than to Lincoln and Guba's working hypothesis framework. Citing this source and explicitly positioning the study's propositions as "working hypotheses" — subject to refinement through the analytical process — would resolve the epistemological tension between the qualitative methodology and the hypothesis-confirmation structure in Chapter 4.

---

### 15. Braun, V. and Clarke, V. (2006) 'Using thematic analysis in psychology', *Qualitative Research in Psychology*, 3(2), pp. 77–101.

**Source Type:** Peer-reviewed journal article (methodological)

**Overview:** This widely cited article provides a systematic account of thematic analysis as a qualitative research method, outlining its theoretical flexibility, its phases of analysis, and the criteria for conducting it rigorously.

**Key Themes:** Braun and Clarke present a six-phase framework for thematic analysis: familiarisation with data, generating initial codes, searching for themes, reviewing themes, defining and naming themes, and producing the report. They emphasise that thematic analysis is a method in its own right, not merely a generic label for "finding themes." The article stresses the importance of making analytical decisions transparent and reflexive.

**Relevance to the Study:** While the dissertation uses Bowen's (2009) document analysis framework, the integration of Braun and Clarke's thematic analysis phases would provide a more transparent and rigorous analytical method for Chapter 4. Specifically, the coding and theme-development stages described by Braun and Clarke would give the revised Chapter 4 the visible analytical process it currently lacks — showing how themes were identified, coded, and refined rather than simply presenting pre-formed conclusions.

## Section E: International Normative Instruments

### 16. Committee on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (2016) *General Comment No. 4 (2016) on Article 24: Right to Inclusive Education* (CRPD/C/GC/4). Geneva: United Nations.

**Source Type:** International legal interpretive instrument

**Overview:** Adopted on 26 August 2016, General Comment No. 4 provides the CRPD Committee's authoritative interpretation of the right to inclusive education under Article 24 of the Convention.

**Key Themes:** General Comment No. 4 distinguishes explicitly between exclusion, segregation, integration, and inclusion, defining inclusion as a process that involves transformation of culture, policy, and practice to accommodate the differing requirements and identities of all students. It clarifies that inclusive education requires systemic reform, not merely placement in mainstream settings. The Committee states that maintaining two systems of education — a mainstream system and a special/segregated system — is not compatible with inclusion. It also identifies core features of the right to inclusive education: availability, accessibility, acceptability, and adaptability.

**Relevance to the Study:** This is a critical normative gap in the current reference list. General Comment No. 4 provides the most authoritative contemporary interpretation of what inclusive education requires under international law, and it postdates both the Salamanca Statement (1994) and the CRPD itself (2006). Its explicit distinction between integration and inclusion directly supports the dissertation's analysis in Section 4.4.2, where Slee's (2011) concept of "integration without inclusion" is deployed. It would strengthen Research Question 1 and Subsidiary Hypothesis 1 by providing an updated normative benchmark.

### 17. Guyana, Parliament of (2010) *Persons with Disabilities Act 2010 (Cap. 36:05)*. Georgetown: Government of Guyana.

**Source Type:** National legislation

**Overview:** The Persons with Disabilities Act 2010 is the primary legislative instrument governing the rights of persons with disabilities in Guyana. It establishes a National Commission on Disabilities and confers specific responsibilities on the Minister of Education regarding the education of children with disabilities.

**Key Themes:** Sub-Part II of the Act mandates that the Ministry of Education: ensure persons with disabilities are not excluded from the general education system; promote integration into regular schools; establish special education classes; facilitate learning through Braille, alternative scripts, and auxiliary aids; and develop training programmes for teachers specialising in disabilities. Critically, this enacted legislation exists alongside the unadopted Draft Inclusion Policy, creating a situation where legislative mandates exist without corresponding operational policy guidance.

**Relevance to the Study:** The Persons with Disabilities Act 2010 is referenced in the annotated bibliography but is not substantively engaged with in the dissertation's analysis. Its provisions create binding legal obligations that coexist with the unadopted Draft Inclusion Policy — a governance incoherence the dissertation identifies (Section 4.4.5) but does not fully analyse. Including this source would strengthen the legal-institutional dimension of the analysis and add a layer of complexity to the governance incoherence finding.

### 18. Guyana, Parliament of (2014) *Education Bill 2014*. Georgetown: Government of Guyana.

**Source Type:** National legislative instrument (Bill)

**Overview:** The 2014 Education Bill provides updated definitions of Special Education Needs that encompass physical, psychological, and sensory impairments as well as giftedness, and mandates the creation of Individual Education Plans in the "least restrictive and most enabling environment."

**Key Themes:** The Bill represents a more recent legislative articulation of educational rights for children with disabilities than either the Persons with Disabilities Act 2010 or the Draft Inclusion Policy. Its definition of SEN is broader and more contemporary than the policy's formulation. However, as confirmed via UNESCO education-profiles.org, inclusive education is not explicitly addressed in the 2014 Education Bill itself.

**Relevance to the Study:** This Bill adds another layer to the governance incoherence analysis in Section 4.4.5 — there are now three instruments (the Draft Inclusion Policy 2010, the Persons with Disabilities Act 2010, and the Education Bill 2014) that address aspects of inclusive education with varying degrees of specificity and legal authority, but without clear coordination or integration. This would strengthen the governance coherence analysis.

### 21. Lipsky, M. (2010) *Street-Level Bureaucracy: Dilemmas of the Individual in Public Services*. 30th anniversary expanded edn. New York: Russell Sage Foundation. (Original work published 1980.)

**Source Type:** Authored book (seminal theoretical text)

**Overview:** This foundational text in public policy theory argues that frontline public service workers — teachers, social workers, police officers — effectively become the real policymakers through their daily discretionary decisions. In contexts where formal policy is absent, ambiguous, or unimplemented, street-level bureaucrats develop coping mechanisms, ration resources, and exercise professional judgement that collectively constitutes the actual policy experienced by citizens.

**Key Themes:** Lipsky demonstrates that the gap between policy intention and implementation outcome is not simply a deficit to be corrected through better top-down management; it is a structural feature of how public services operate. Frontline workers facing high demand, inadequate resources, and ambiguous directives inevitably simplify, prioritise, and adapt their responses in ways that may diverge significantly from official policy. The concept of "street-level bureaucracy" has become one of the most widely cited frameworks in policy implementation research.

**Relevance to the Study:** This is an essential addition identified as a critical gap by external verification. The dissertation currently explains the policy-practice gap entirely through top-down models (Pressman and Wildavsky, Matland). Lipsky provides the necessary bottom-up complement. In the Guyanese context, where the Draft Inclusion Policy has not been formally adopted, teachers and SEN officers are effectively the de facto policymakers — their daily decisions about how to accommodate (or fail to accommodate) learners with disabilities constitute the actual inclusive education "policy" as experienced. The 51 CPCE SEND graduates and the establishment of 30 SEND spaces represent precisely the kind of bottom-up, localised implementation that Lipsky's framework predicts in the absence of centralised policy direction. This source should be integrated into the theoretical framework (Chapter 2), the analysis of structural factors (Section 4.4), and the refined hypothesis evaluations (Section 4.5). It is relevant to Research Questions 2 and 3 and to the Central Hypothesis and Subsidiary Hypothesis 3.

### 22. UNESCO (2020) *Global Education Monitoring Report 2020: Inclusion and Education — All Means All*. Paris: UNESCO.

**Source Type:** International organisation report

**Overview:** This comprehensive report examines inclusion and education globally, providing data, analysis, and policy recommendations on how education systems can become more inclusive. It covers disability, gender, poverty, ethnicity, language, migration, and other dimensions of exclusion.

**Key Themes:** The report argues that inclusion requires systemic transformation rather than targeted interventions for specific groups. It provides specific metrics and qualitative analyses of how developing countries manage policy implementation gaps, unadopted policy drafts, and the challenges of coordinating across siloed government ministries. The report establishes that an estimated 258 million children and youth worldwide were entirely excluded from education, with disability being one of the most significant factors in exclusion.

**Relevance to the Study:** This source modernises the normative framework for Research Question 1. The dissertation currently evaluates the Draft Inclusion Policy against the Salamanca Statement (1994) and the CRPD (2006). The GEM Report 2020 provides the most authoritative contemporary account of what inclusive education requires in practice, aligned with SDG 4 (inclusive and equitable quality education). It is directly relevant to updating the normative benchmarks against which the policy is assessed and to strengthening the evidence base for the recommendations in Chapter 5.

### 23. Abdul-Hamid, H. (2014) *What Matters Most for Education Management Information Systems: A Framework Paper*. SABER Working Paper Series, No. 7. Washington, DC: World Bank.

**Source Type:** International organisation working paper

**Overview:** This framework paper examines how Education Management Information Systems (EMIS) function as tools for educational accountability, resource allocation, and policy monitoring in developing countries. It provides a structured framework for assessing the maturity and effectiveness of national education data systems.

**Key Themes:** Abdul-Hamid argues that effective education policy implementation depends on robust data collection, analysis, and feedback mechanisms. Without functioning EMIS, ministries of education cannot track enrolment, attendance, completion, or learning outcomes for specific populations — including learners with disabilities. The paper identifies the specific components of effective EMIS and the conditions under which they succeed or fail in resource-constrained environments.

**Relevance to the Study:** This source fills the critical gap identified in monitoring and evaluation (M&E) literature. Subsidiary Hypothesis 3 explicitly identifies "the lack of a systematic monitoring and accountability architecture" as a structural constraint, yet the current dissertation contains no theoretical literature on M&E systems. Abdul-Hamid's framework provides the theoretical grounding necessary for the M&E-related recommendations in Chapter 5, transforming them from generic best-practice assertions into theoretically supported proposals. It is directly relevant to Research Questions 3 and 4 and to Subsidiary Hypothesis 3.

### 24. Norwich, B. (2016) 'Conceptualizing special educational needs using a biopsychosocial model in England: The prospects and challenges of using the International Classification of Functioning framework', *Frontiers in Education*, 1, Article 5.

**Source Type:** Peer-reviewed journal article

**Overview:** This article examines how the biopsychosocial model, operationalised through the WHO's International Classification of Functioning (ICF) framework, can be applied to the conceptualisation of special educational needs within a national education system. Norwich uses England as a case study but develops generalisable analytical categories.

**Key Themes:** Norwich provides a concrete, education-specific methodology for evaluating whether a policy document's underlying conception of disability aligns with the biopsychosocial model or remains anchored in older medical or social model assumptions. The article demonstrates how the ICF's dimensions — body functions and structures, activities, participation, and environmental factors — can be used as coding categories for policy text analysis.

**Relevance to the Study:** This source addresses the gap in education-specific application of the biopsychosocial model. The dissertation currently uses the biopsychosocial model as an analytical lens but lacks a source that demonstrates how to apply it specifically to educational policy analysis. Norwich provides exactly this methodology — a framework for coding and evaluating policy text against biopsychosocial dimensions. This would strengthen the theoretical framework in Chapter 2 and provide more rigorous analytical categories for the application of the biopsychosocial lens in Chapter 4.

### 25. Shields, P.M., Rangarajan, N. and Casula, M. (2019) 'It is a working hypothesis: Searching for truth in a post-truth world', *Foundations of Science*, 24, pp. 187–211.

**Source Type:** Peer-reviewed journal article

**Overview:** This article examines the concept of the working hypothesis as a methodological tool in social science research, tracing its intellectual history from Dewey's pragmatism through Lincoln and Guba's naturalistic inquiry to contemporary applications in qualitative and mixed-methods research.

**Key Themes:** Shields et al. argue that the working hypothesis functions as a micro-conceptual framework linking qualitative data to deductive or abductive conclusions. Unlike formal hypotheses in positivist research, working hypotheses are explicitly provisional, designed to be refined through engagement with evidence, and understood as context-bound rather than universally generalisable. The article provides contemporary methodological justification for the use of working hypotheses in qualitative research designs.

**Relevance to the Study:** This source complements Lincoln and Guba (1985) by providing a contemporary (2019) defence of the working hypothesis methodology. While Lincoln and Guba remain the foundational citation, relying solely on a 1985 text to justify the core methodological mechanism of a 2026 dissertation is a vulnerability. Shields et al. demonstrate that the working hypothesis remains a live, methodologically rigorous concept in current social science, strengthening the methodological defence in Chapter 3.
