# Writing Style Analysis — Benchmark from Published Academic Work

## Purpose

This document analyses the stylistic fingerprints of the author's published academic writing (Persaud et al., 2021, Hydrobiologia). Claude Code should use this as the target voice when rewriting the dissertation. The goal is to adopt the **stylistic patterns** (sentence construction, vocabulary habits, paragraph rhythm, analytical voice) WITHOUT importing any subject-matter content, jargon, or concepts from this paper. The paper is about paleolimnology and Cladocera — none of that should appear in the dissertation.

---

## 1. SENTENCE-LEVEL PATTERNS

### 1.1 Sentence Length Variation

The author exhibits strong burstiness. Sentence lengths range considerably within paragraphs:

- Short sentences (8–15 words): Used for stating key facts or findings plainly. E.g., "This study provides insights into ecological change in Yellowknife lakes." (~10 words)
- Medium sentences (16–25 words): The most common type, used for standard analytical statements.
- Long sentences (26–45 words): Used for complex methodological descriptions, multi-clause comparisons, or sentences with embedded citations. E.g., "The primary objectives of our study were to: (1) reconstruct regional changes in cladoceran assemblages since pre-industrial times; and (2) identify cladoceran taxa that appear to be sensitive or tolerant of arsenic, which can be explored as candidate ecological indicators of arsenic pollution in future research." (~46 words)

**Key pattern:** The author does NOT maintain uniform sentence length. Within a single paragraph, sentences range from ~10 to ~45 words. This is excellent for anti-AI detection.

### 1.2 Sentence Openers

The author uses varied sentence openers:

- Subject-first declarative: "Cladocera are a superorder of small crustaceans..."
- Prepositional phrase: "In the subarctic City of Yellowknife..."
- Adverbial opener: "Importantly, in mining regions where..."
- Temporal/locational: "From 1948 to the cessation of roasting in 1999..."
- Participial/dependent clause: "While not well researched for Cladocera..."
- Citation-led: "Thienpont et al. (2016) examined..."
- Demonstrative (but varied): "This study..." / "These findings..." / "The exception..."
- Concessive: "However, the positive relationship..."
- Conditional: "If the positive relationship between species richness/diversity and [As] is direct..."

**Key pattern:** The author does NOT start more than 2 consecutive sentences with the same word. "This" and "The" are used but rotated with other openers.

### 1.3 Sentence Complexity

The author mixes:
- Simple declarative sentences for key findings.
- Compound sentences joined by semicolons (fairly frequent — a stylistic preference).
- Complex sentences with subordinate clauses for nuanced points.
- Sentences with parenthetical asides set off by commas or parentheses for methodological notes.

**Notable habit:** The author uses semicolons to join related independent clauses within a single sentence, rather than using coordinating conjunctions. This is a distinguishing stylistic marker.

### 1.4 Vocabulary Preferences

The author's vocabulary is **precise and technical without being ornate.** Key characteristics:

- Uses concrete, specific terms over abstract generalisations.
- Prefers plain verbs: "showed," "found," "examined," "documented," "noted," "observed," "reported," "attributed," "suggested," "indicated."
- Rarely uses evaluative adjectives like "crucial," "pivotal," "comprehensive," "robust."
- When evaluation is needed, uses restrained language: "relatively tolerant," "comparatively higher," "not well researched," "poorly understood," "notably."
- Uses disciplinary jargon naturally (without announcing it).
- Does NOT use: delve, landscape (metaphorically), tapestry, multifaceted, nuanced (as adjective), holistic, framework (excessively), operationalise, architecture (metaphorically).

**Favourite words/phrases (idiosyncratic returns):**
- "notably" (used several times — a genuine authorial preference)
- "clearly" (used once or twice for emphasis)
- "as such" (used as a transition)
- "however" (frequent but positioned mid-sentence or at sentence start, varied)
- "despite" (used for concessive structures)
- "given that" / "as such" / "in contrast" (preferred transition devices)

### 1.5 Passive vs. Active Voice

The author uses a **natural mix:**
- Active voice for reporting findings: "We found that..." / "We identified..." / "We used..."
- Passive voice for methodology: "Sediment cores were collected..." / "Water samples were filtered..."
- Passive voice for describing previous research: "has been documented," "have been attributed"

**Key pattern:** The author does NOT maintain a consistent passive rate. It shifts based on whether the author is describing their own actions (active) vs. established facts (passive).

## 2. PARAGRAPH-LEVEL PATTERNS

### 2.1 Paragraph Length Variation

The author's paragraphs vary considerably:
- Short paragraphs (3–4 sentences): Used for stating specific findings or methodological details.
- Medium paragraphs (5–7 sentences): The most common, used for developed analytical points.
- Long paragraphs (8–12 sentences): Used for literature review synthesis or complex methodological descriptions.

**Key pattern:** Paragraph length is NOT uniform. Some paragraphs are half the length of others, even within the same section.

### 2.2 Paragraph Internal Structure

The author does NOT follow a rigid formula. Paragraphs begin with:
- A finding or claim (most common).
- A reference to previous research.
- A concession or contrast with what was just discussed.
- A methodological statement.

**Key pattern:** Not every paragraph has the same internal structure. Some start with evidence, some with interpretation, some with context.

### 2.3 Paragraph Transitions

Transitions are **mostly implicit** — the author relies on logical flow rather than explicit transition words. When explicit transitions are used, they are varied:
- "However,..." (for contrast)
- "In addition to..." (for addition — but NOT "Furthermore" or "Moreover")
- "As such,..." (for consequence)
- "Notably,..." (for emphasis)
- "In contrast,..." (for comparison)

**Key pattern:** The author does NOT use "Furthermore," "Moreover," or "Additionally" as paragraph openers. These are AI-typical transitions. The author prefers "However," "In contrast," "As such," and implicit connections.

## 3. ANALYTICAL VOICE

### 3.1 Evaluative Stance

The author's evaluative voice is **cautious but direct:**
- States findings plainly without excessive hedging.
- Uses conditional language when genuinely uncertain: "may be," "suggests that," "possibly," "it is possible that."
- Does NOT stack hedges. A single qualifier per claim is typical.
- Acknowledges limitations directly: "it is possible that very rare species may not have been identified, lowering the calculated diversity value."
- Uses "however" to introduce genuine complications, not performative balance.

### 3.2 Engagement with Counterevidence

The author addresses counterevidence naturally:
- States the complicating finding.
- Offers possible explanations.
- Acknowledges what remains uncertain.
- Does NOT force resolution — comfortable leaving questions open.

Example pattern: "However, in Cobalt (Ontario), Bosmina were reported in lakes with surface water arsenic concentrations as high as 1110 μg/l. Geographic variation in genetic background, the bioavailability and form of arsenic to which the taxa is exposed, and the influence of other water quality variables... can act collectively to influence species ecotoxicological responses to arsenic concentration across different regions."

### 3.3 Specificity

The author is **highly specific:**
- Cites exact concentrations, exact sample sizes, exact statistical values.
- References specific lakes by name, specific taxa by binomial nomenclature.
- Provides exact methodological details (temperatures, volumes, magnification levels).
- Does NOT make vague generalisations without specific supporting data.

## 4. DOCUMENT-LEVEL PATTERNS

### 4.1 Section Structure

- Sections follow disciplinary conventions but are not over-announced.
- The author does NOT begin sections by saying "This section will..." and end by saying "This section has shown..."
- Introductory context is provided concisely before moving to the substance.

### 4.2 Citation Integration

The author integrates citations naturally:
- Author-date format in running text.
- Sometimes citation-led: "Thienpont et al. (2016) examined..."
- Sometimes evidence-led: "...have been attributed to regional climate warming (Leppänen et al., 2017)."
- Multiple citations grouped parenthetically when supporting a general claim.
- Does NOT announce citations: avoids "According to Smith (2020)..." repeatedly.

### 4.3 Tone

- Professional, measured, and precise.
- Not dry or robotic — the author's engagement with the material comes through in the level of analytical detail and the careful handling of complexity.
- Not enthusiastic or promotional — avoids "groundbreaking," "revolutionary," "cutting-edge."
- Comfortable with uncertainty — uses phrases like "remains unclear," "poorly understood," "further research is needed."

---

## 5. SUMMARY: STYLE FINGERPRINT FOR REWRITING

When rewriting dissertation text to match this author's voice:

1. **Vary sentence lengths dramatically** within paragraphs (10–45 words).
2. **Use plain, precise verbs:** found, showed, examined, documented, observed, noted, reported, suggested, indicated. Avoid ornate alternatives.
3. **Vary sentence openers:** Rotate between subject-first, prepositional, adverbial, citation-led, concessive, and conditional openers.
4. **Use semicolons** to join related clauses (a genuine authorial habit).
5. **Avoid AI-typical transitions:** No "Furthermore," "Moreover," "Additionally" as paragraph openers. Use "However," "In contrast," "As such," or implicit transitions.
6. **Be specific:** Include concrete details — page references, provision numbers, exact wordings from primary documents.
7. **Don't stack qualifiers.** One hedge per claim is enough.
8. **Vary paragraph lengths** (3–12 sentences).
9. **Don't announce what sections will do.** Just do it.
10. **Leave some questions open.** Not every analytical point needs resolution.
11. **Avoid evaluative adjective clusters:** No "crucial," "pivotal," "comprehensive," "robust," "multifaceted," "nuanced" — use restraint.
12. **Keep transitions natural.** Many paragraph transitions should be implicit.
